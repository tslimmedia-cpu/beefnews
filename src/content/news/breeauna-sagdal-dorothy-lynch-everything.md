**As a food writer who focuses on the Midwest, I am frequently accused of writing things for "shock value"—a tacit indictment that my work holds no other kind.**

This is offensive, of course, and it used to offend me. The things I write about—[hot beef sundaes](https://midwesterner.substack.com/p/tasteless-beef-a-la-mode), [pizza ranch](https://haterade.substack.com/p/pizza-ranch)—are things I love. Midwestern food is one of the last sturdy projection screens for other people's disgust, and I used to waste hours seething about it, shadowboxing imaginary opponents to defend its worth.

Then, I decided to take a cue from Dorothy Lynch.

The company behind the tomato-soup-based, celery-laced, not-quite-French dressing is proudly Nebraskan, but with a global—some might say aspirational—view of its own appeal. It's easy to love Dorothy on Nebraska taco salads and chilled bowls of iceberg. As Jed wrote earlier this week, it's also great on [wings](https://midwesterner.substack.com/p/happy-birthday-dorothy-lynch) and as a muse for [red beer](https://midwesterner.substack.com/p/the-dorothy-lynch-red-beer). But the Dorothy Lynch [website](https://www.dorothylynch.com/recipes) has a compendium of demented recipes that would make even a part-time food snob blanch.

The website tagline is "Endless Flavorbilities," a clear sign that they are not fucking around. Tasty Toppings, Inc. has never considered that you might be closed off to a flavorbility, or they've decided not to care. Remember how everyone freaked out when NYT Cooking published a guacamole recipe that included [peas](https://slate.com/culture/2015/07/new-york-times-pea-guacamole-controversy-a-deep-analysis-of-the-tweet-that-outraged-america.html?pay=1776190946635&support_journalism=please)? Imagine [guacamole with Dorothy Lynch](https://www.dorothylynch.com/recipes/guacamole-dorothy-lynch-home-style), a recipe the company has been pushing for more than a decade.

There is no way to make fun of Dorothy Lynch; she has outwitted you at every turn. Try to imagine a recipe you could squeeze the salad dressing into for comic effect. It will never be as funny as "[Crabmeat Tomato Accordions](https://www.dorothylynch.com/recipes/crabmeat-tomato-accordions)."

Perhaps you're thinking you could push the envelope with a dessert. Like Dorothy Lynch [brownies](https://www.dorothylynch.com/recipes/dorothy-lynch-brownies)? Or a spiced mandarin orange [bundt cake](https://www.dorothylynch.com/recipes/spiced-mandarin-orange-bundt-cake) that earned an Honorable Mention at the 2012 Iowa State Fair?

What I admire most about this collection of recipes is that there's not a whiff of self-consciousness in any of them. The recipe writer takes for granted that a Dorothy Lynch cake could be delicious, and that you might want to make one.

And why wouldn't you? Dorothy's tomato soup base makes it a reasonable analogue for the namesake ingredient in tomato soup cake, a Depression-era invention that relied on condensed tomato soup to tenderize cakes when dairy was hard to come by. The name raises eyebrows now, but 100 years ago, it was ubiquitous. The tomato added acidity in the vein of buttermilk, but it mostly added moisture. Historical reviewers rarely remarked on the tomato—they wrote instead about how the cake tasted like gingerbread or fudge.

Even M. F. K. Fisher liked tomato soup cake. In her 1941 book _How to Cook A Wolf, _she describes it as "a pleasant cake, which keeps well and puzzles people while you are cooking other things, which is always sensible and makes you feel rather noble."

Tomato soup cake represents one of home economists' great historical acts of alchemy. And yet, every couple of years, a tweet or Reddit post or [video](https://www.youtube.com/shorts/v8sYkwhnujA) of an old tomato soup cake recipe goes viral—not because we appreciate the magic, but because we've forgotten how to look for it. The idea alone makes us recoil. The shock value is the only kind we see.

This is a parable, of course, about Midwestern food.

So in honor of Dorothy Lynch's birthday, here's my commitment: I'm not wasting any more time defending regional foods from incurious people. The next time someone comments "no." on one of my recipes or mocks a #flavorbility they haven't deigned to contemplate, I'm giving them the culinary equivalent of the Gen Z stare.

The spirit of the Midwest, if we are brave enough to embody it, is a joyful, gremlin-like pursuit of innovation that is both unprecious and completely sincere. This is a rare gift, and one we relinquish when we contort ourselves to avoid critique.

In that spirit, I bring you a new recipe for Dorothy Lynch Cake, developed after studying decades of tomato soup cake recipes as a template. The resulting cake is a natural descendent that bakes up with a blush of tomato-celery flavor but a surprisingly light and tender crumb.

I'm not going to exaggerate its deliciousness, or engage in the recipe developer charade of pretending it's part of my regular rotation (_"My Family Begs For This Cake Every Weekend. They Don't Know My Secret Ingredient.") _I–Dorothy–the Midwest–don't need the fanfare. It's enough to be practical and humble and good.

It's a pleasant cake, and it keeps well.

## **DOROTHY LYNCH CAKE**

_Makes 1 cake, serving 10–12
Prep time: 15 minutes
Cook time: 45 minutes
Total time: 1 hour, plus cooling_

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!vv19!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F19036499-9a64-4a7c-a86e-edaae04c1587_4615x3461.png)

](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!vv19!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F19036499-9a64-4a7c-a86e-edaae04c1587_4615x3461.png)
**Ingredients**
 cup (1 stick; 113 g) unsalted butter, at cool room temperature, plus more for greasing the bundt pan
2 cups (300 g) all-purpose flour, plus more for greasing the bundt pan
1 cup (207 g) granulated sugar
 tsp. baking soda
1 tsp. baking powder
 tsp. Diamond Crystal kosher salt (if using table salt, use half as much)
2 eggs, at room temperature
1 cups (350 g) Dorothy Lynch or [Not Quite Dorothy Lynch](https://midwesterner.substack.com/p/happy-birthday-dorothy-lynch) dressing
Powdered sugar, for topping

**Preparation
**Preheat the oven to 350F. Generously butter and flour one 9-inch bundt pan.

Add butter, sugar, salt, baking powder, and baking soda to a large mixing bowl. Using an electric hand mixer, beat together them on high until mixture is light and fluffy, 5–7 minutes. Add eggs and beat on low speed until combined.

Add, in alternating order, flour (in three parts) and Dorothy (in two), ending with flour. Mix on low in between each addition, until flour and dressing are fully incorporated. The batter will be thick.

Using a rubber spatula, scrape the batter into the prepared bundt pan and spread until evenly distributed and relatively level in the pan. Bake for 35–45 minutes, or until a toothpick inserted near the center comes out clean.

Let the cake cool in the pan for 5 minutes, then invert it on a wire rack, remove the pan, and allow it to cool fully before cutting. Dust the cake with powdered sugar immediately before serving.
