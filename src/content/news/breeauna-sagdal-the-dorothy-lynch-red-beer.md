**Maybe the Great Plains red beer evolved from the Mexican michelada**. Maybe the idea of combining tomato juice with fizzy yellow lager, for a lighter version of the Bloody Mary that you can sip all day while you watch sports, is just obvious.

"I've never bothered to look into the origin of it," says Jordan Elfers of [O'Rourke's Tavern](https://www.facebook.com/orourkestavern/) in Lincoln, Nebraska, a University of Nebraska haunt since 1980. But he guesses the bar serves at least one of the Cornhusker-red concoctions every day, and many more during football season.

The drink, also called a tomato beer, bloody beer, red draw, and red eye, isn't just a Nebraska thing, but it's become a statewide tradition.

"Certainly, anyone who's been to a Nebraska football game, which is a large portion of the state, has seen it at some point," says Matthew Hansen, executive editor of the [Flatwater Free Press](https://flatwaterfreepress.org/). "It's a tailgate-friendly, morning or early afternoon type of drink. The other place I see it most is on the golf course. I don't know if I've ever seen anyone drinking one at 7 p.m."

If you aren't from the Plains, you can think of red beer as a savory cousin to the more popular [beermosa](https://www.liquor.com/recipes/beermosa/), made from beer and orange juice, which Elfers has heard called a "California red beer" around Lincoln.

Both drinks, said to be morning tonics and hangover cures, are usually made from about one part juice and three parts beer, though that can vary widely by bartender and drinker, and they're almost never measured out that precisely. "The only rule I have for red beer is that I wouldn't suggest putting red in an IPA," Elfers says. The base should be neutral, like the vodka in a Bloody. "But I've done it for a customer."

Whether or not the Plains invented red beer, Nebraska is one of the few states where you can confidently order one, trusting that the bartender will at least know what you mean, and probably pull out a jug of Campbell's or V8.

It's been that way for at least 75 years, if not longer. In 1950, responding to a wide-eyed piece about "bloody beer" in the Omaha _World-Herald_, A.H. Ewert of the David City, Nebraska, _People's Banner _wrote: "The tomato beer has been around and out this way for years. No one thinks of it as a strange thing."

Besides the color it shares with Nebraska's football uniforms, the best explanation for red beer's enduring appeal on the Plains is that tomato juice pairs well with the region's best-selling drafts.

"For your average Nebraskan, domestic light beer is still the number-one option," Hansen says. "Light beer isn't really my thing, but the tomato juice gives it more heft and taste. I might not take a Bud Light, but I'll take a _red_ Bud Light." That isn't craft beer–era judgment. The sentiment's been around for generations. Back in 1962, a man at a bar in North Platte, Nebraska, snapped to another that he was using tomato juice to "kill the beer taste," per a report in the city's _Telegraph-Bulletin_. Like other flavor-maxxing Nebraskans, Hansen also raids tailgate Bloody Mary bars for fixings, adding the likes of olives, pickles, hot sauce, and lime to his cup.

_But wait,_ you're thinking, _what does this have to do with Dorothy Lynch?_ Or maybe you read the title and can tell where this is going.

Pouring tomato juice into light beer has universal appeal. Why not tomato soup–based salad dressing?

I can't be the only person who's tried mixing two of Nebraska's tomato-based favorites, but my first experiment helped me understand why I couldn't find any recipes. It was thick, an unsettlingly milky shade of orange, and Shirley Temple–sweet. Dorothy _can_ work in almost anything, including a Bloody Mary, but it wasn't the right standalone mixer for beer, and I didn't want to overcomplicate things.

Fortunately, [I'd already reverse-engineered Dorothy for Monday's piece](https://midwesterner.substack.com/p/happy-birthday-dorothy-lynch), so I could pull out the dressing's definitive ingredients and add them to tomato juice, making an almost-classic red beer that tastes like the best of both traditions.

In general, red beer is a less-is-more kind of drink, meant to be splashed into pint glasses at sports bars and in parking lots. I don't think adding the flavors of Nebraska's most famous salad dressing makes it _better_, exactly. But this is one souped-up beer that could only come from the Cornhusker State.

## **DOROTHY LYNCH-SPICED RED BEER**

_Makes 3–4_ _drinks
Total time: 5 minutes, plus a 4-hour to overnight fridge rest_

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!PyVn!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F1f44411a-5764-4a74-a981-20e1c3a89f75_2070x1552.jpeg)

](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!PyVn!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F1f44411a-5764-4a74-a981-20e1c3a89f75_2070x1552.jpeg)
Working on this recipe, I prioritized making a drinkable red beer over a Dorothy Lynch clone. Keep in mind that the mix will be seriously diluted in the beer. The final product won't be intensely flavored or too sweet.

The generous amount of celery seed, necessary for a Dorothy-like flavor profile, adds some grit. That's no big deal in a Bloody Mary, and it doesn't bother me. But for or the smoothest texture, you can strain the juice through a fine-mesh sieve after the fridge rest. Because tomato juice is thick, it'll take a while, but you'll end up with most of the flavor and none of the floating spices. (I tested it.) You will lose some mix, so expect to get 2-3 drinks from this recipe rather than 3-4.

**Ingredients
**1 11.5-oz can tomato juice, such as Campbell's (about 1 cups)
2 tbsp. (25 g) granulated sugar
2 tbsp. (30 ml) apple cider vinegar
1 tsp. celery seed
1 tsp. prepared yellow mustard, such as French's
 tsp. onion powder
 tsp. freshly ground black pepper
 tsp. Diamond Crystal kosher salt (if using table salt, use half as much)
3–4 12-oz. (355 ml) cold cans of light lager

**Preparation
**Whisk or shake tomato juice, sugar, vinegar, celery seed, mustard, onion powder, black pepper, and salt in a quart-sized container with a lid until sugar dissolves. 

Refrigerate for at least 4 hours, and preferably overnight, to let the flavors bloom. 

If you're straining, do it after the rest: Use a fine-mesh sieve—not cheesecloth or a coffee filter, which will clog quickly—and allow plenty of time.

To serve, pour a big splash of the tomato juice mixture into a pint glass. Top with beer. Give it a quick stir. Serve immediately.
