**Forget pumpkin spice. **In the Midwest, black walnut is the real flavor of fall.

If you've been reading _Midwesterner _for a while, you already know I think so. Since 2020, I've walked you through [the way I process black walnuts](https://midwesterner.org/editors-letter-black-walnut-season/) and how to turn them into [black walnut butter](https://midwesterner.org/how-to-make-black-walnut-butter/), [black walnut cake](https://midwesterner.substack.com/p/midwestern-luxury-black-walnut-cake), and [black walnut nocino](https://midwesterner.org/how-to-make-black-walnut-nocino/) ([twice](https://midwesterner.org/editors-letter-nocino-season/)).

Maybe, like me, you've been inspired to order a [Grandpa's Goody Getter](https://midwesterner.org/a-better-way-to-crack-a-black-walnut/), a Missouri-made nutcracker that's built to break stubborn shells. Or maybe, also like me, you've basically given up on the frustrating, finger-staining work of handling your own black walnuts, and you just buy them from [Hammons Black Walnuts](https://black-walnuts.com), [a company we profiled back in 2021](https://midwesterner.substack.com/p/stockton-missouri-is-the-black-walnut). (If you value your time and peace of mind, it's a better deal.)

There's still plenty more to say about the yellow-green treasures piling up in yard waste bags across the Midwest. That's why _Midwesterner_ is all about black walnuts this week. Today, Wednesday, and Friday, you'll get stories and recipes celebrating the region's most distinctive native nut. And because everything feels more special when it's tied up in a package, I'm calling it Black Walnut Week.

To start, I wanted to go back to basics. For help with that, I reached out to Hammons, America's only large-scale black walnut processor, based in Stockton, Missouri. (For more on that, [read our profile](https://midwesterner.substack.com/p/stockton-missouri-is-the-black-walnut).) Hammons VP Jacob Basecke, the great-grandson of company founder Ralph Hammons, agreed to help me out with some FAQs.

And if you're already thinking ahead to the holidays, skip to the end to get a Hammons family–inspired recipe for a classic casserole with a toasty walnut topping.

**What's the difference between black walnuts and the English walnuts at the grocery store?**

Those New World and Old World nuts are separated by more than just an ocean. "The biggest misconception about the American black walnut is that it's just a stronger version of the English walnut," Basecke says. "It's actually an entirely different species, with its own flavor profile—rich, woodsy, and aromatic."

That difference, he says, is a constant source of confusion. "People think walnuts are walnuts, and we're constantly having to educate folks that, no, these are different." Sometimes, he says, he wishes black walnuts weren't called walnuts at all. "It would make our job a lot easier."

**Okay. Tell me more about that flavor profile. What do black walnuts taste like?**

"Bold" is a word that comes up often. "Earthy" is another. In past editions of _Midwesterner_, we've called out notes of bubblegum, grapefruit, green grass, and—one of my favorites—"damp forest." (Thanks to chef Josh Galliano.)

Black walnuts _are_ more assertive than mild, buttery English walnuts, with a bitterness that can surprise first-timers. "They're not subtle," Basecke says. "You either instantly love them or do a double take." That strong flavor has a way of permeating anything the nuts are in, whether it's cookies, cakes, or cheese balls.

For people who grew up with black walnuts, they taste like home. "Customers often tell us that black walnuts taste like their grandmother's kitchen," Basecke says. "It's a nostalgic flavor."

**If I've never tried anything made with black walnuts, where should I start?**

"I'd start with banana bread," Basecke says. "It's simple, it's easy to make, and the flavor of black walnut balances well with the other ingredients. Apple cake is another favorite, especially during the fall, and a sweet potato casserole is always a crowd-pleaser."

I'd also recommend ice cream. Black walnut was one of Baskin-Robbins' original thirty-one flavors. It still has a following, though a friend recently (and, I'm afraid, correctly) told me that he considers it an "old man" flavor. A while back, I was concerned to see that it was no longer in the case at United Dairy Farmers, a chain of convenience stores with ice cream parlors in my hometown of Cincinnati. It's my go-to for malts. "Oh, we've got it," the cashier reassured me. There was a container under the counter for customers like me—which is to say, with less fashionable tastes. 

"It's an old ice cream flavor, but it's coming back," Basecke says, citing the new-wave New York creamery Morgenstern's, [which paired black walnut with the bartender-favorite amaro Fernet Branca in an ice cream](https://www.grubstreet.com/2018/10/morgensterns-nyc-flagship-88-flavors.html). Missouri's Hiland Dairy [just released a black walnut-and-caramel ice cream called Ozark Harvest Crunch](https://hilanddairy.com/media-center/hiland-dairy-and-hammons-black-walnuts-team-up-to-introduce-ozark-harvest-crunch-ice-cream/).

**What else can you make with them?**

"People have gotten really creative with black walnuts over the years," Basecke says. Most home cooks stick to desserts, but chefs, bartenders, and other culinary professionals have gone much further. "We've seen black walnut used as a crust for duck, fish, and pork, and chefs adding that deep, earthy flavor to pestos and other sauces," he says. Pennsylvania's Keepwell Vinegar sells a [black walnut miso](https://www.keepwellvinegar.com/shop/white-miso).

The nut is a taste of place across the Midwest, South, and Mid-Atlantic, but it's especially popular in Hammons' home state of Missouri. Piney River Brewing Company, in Bucyrus, brews [an award-winning dark wheat ale](https://pineyriverbrewing.com/beer/black-walnut/) with Hammons nuts. St Louis' Taiwanese-inspired [Foundry Bakery](https://www.thefoundrybakery.com/) kneads black walnuts into a sourdough loaf. In Kansas City, [Fox & Pearl](https://www.foxandpearlkc.com) is dressing a fall salad with a black walnut vinaigrette.

Hammons has gotten creative over the years, too. The company sells a [cooking oil](https://www.hammonspantry.com/collections/wild-black-walnuts/products/black-walnut-oil-8-4oz-bottle) pressed from roasted black walnuts and mills the leftovers into a [protein-rich flour](https://www.hammonspantry.com/collections/pantry-staples/products/black-walnut-protein-powder). It recently introduced [HIKE](https://www.hammonspantry.com/collections/hike-performance-snacks), a line of trail mixes built around black walnuts. "I hope we can continue building that out, maybe with bars and things like that," Basecke says.

**Are there black walnut orchards out there somewhere?**

Yes, but that's not where most of the nuts are coming from. "There are landowners who've planted black walnut orchards, but that's less than one percent of our total crop," Basecke says. "Depending on the year, probably less than half a percent."

The rest come from the company's [170 hulling stations in twelve states](https://black-walnuts.com/discover-harvesting-and-hulling/hulling-and-buying-locations/), where farmers and other amateur foragers sell heaps of wild nuts that they've collected from the ground. "They haul them in buckets, truck beds, whatever they've got," Basecke says. Suppliers currently get $13 for each 100 pounds (after hulling).

The hulled nuts are then trucked to Hammons headquarters in Stockton, where they're washed, cured, cracked, picked, and sorted before going out to retailers nationwide.

Because Hammons' black walnut harvest is almost entirely wild, it can vary dramatically from year to year. A good season might bring fifteen million pounds of nuts—a bad one, half that. "We're at the mercy of the weather," Basecke says. The company's record stands at forty-nine million pounds, in 1999. Their worst year? Four million. This year, the company decided to extend the buying season, which began on October 1, into mid-November, to account for a warm fall that delayed the drop.

**Let's say I have a black walnut tree in the yard, and I'm just now finding out these nuts are good for something. What are the steps I need to take to get from tree to table?**

For a thorough rundown, [read our piece from 2020](https://midwesterner.org/editors-letter-black-walnut-season/). 

Here's the short version: Wait until the nuts fall. Gather them from the ground while the husks are still green, before they blacken or mold. (The hulls stain everything they touch, so wear gloves through the whole process. I had a black thumb for _weeks_ after two layers of latex glove broke while I was wrist-deep in a bucket of walnuts.)

To remove the husks, you might stomp them, whack them with a hammer, or run them over with a car. Wash the hulled nuts well, then spread them out to dry in a cool place with good air circulation for a couple of weeks. After that, you'll have crack the thick shells with a heavy-duty nutcracker—Basecke also recommends [Grandpa's Goody Getter](https://midwesterner.org/a-better-way-to-crack-a-black-walnut/)—and pick out the nutmeats.

"It's a lot of work," Basecke says. "That's why we tell people, just let us do it for you."

## **BLACK WALNUT SWEET POTATO CASSEROLE
**_Adapted from Hammons Black Walnuts, Stockton, Missouri_

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!8cib!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F79892ce0-dc54-43c3-b00e-3bac04ea8165_4032x2111.png)

](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!8cib!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F79892ce0-dc54-43c3-b00e-3bac04ea8165_4032x2111.png)
_Serves 6–8_
_Prep time: 30 minutes
Cook time: 2 hours (including 30 minutes of cooling)
Total time: 3 hours_

"There's never a shortage of black walnuts at our family dinners," Basecke says, though you couldn't blame the family for wanting a break every now and then. "In fact, my grandpa Dwain Hammons, who ran the company for years, would put an extra handful of black walnuts on top of his desserts, for more of that flavor."

Like many families, the Hammons and Baseckes make sweet potato casserole for the holidays. This is my version of a company recipe, with a custard-like sweet potato filling and a crisp black walnut streusel topping.

**Ingredients**

_Filling_**
**4 lb. (1.8 kg) sweet potatoes (4 or 5 large)
 cup (55 g /  stick) unsalted butter, melted and slightly cooled
 cup (100 g) light brown sugar
1 tsp. Diamond Crystal kosher salt (for table salt, use half as much)
 cup (120 g) half-and-half (you can substitute whole milk or heavy cream)_
_2 large eggs (100 g)
1 tsp. high-quality vanilla extract_
_1 tsp. ground cinnamon
 tsp. ground nutmeg
 tsp. ground clove

_Topping_**
** cup (40 g) all-purpose flour
 cup (30 g) old-fashioned rolled oats
 cup (70 g) packed brown sugar
1 cup (100 g) black walnut pieces, chopped into pea-sized bits or pulsed in a food processor
 tsp. ground cinnamon
Generous pinch Diamond Crystal kosher salt
 cup (55 g /  stick) unsalted butter, softened

**Preparation
**Preheat the oven to 350F (175C). Prick the sweet potatoes all over with a fork and place them on a parchment-lined baking sheet. Bake for 1 hours, or until a butter knife slides in easily. Let cool until they can be handled, about 30 minutes.

Cut the sweet potatoes in half and scoop the flesh into a large mixing bowl. Add butter, brown sugar, salt, half-and-half, eggs, vanilla, and spices. Beat with an electric mixer or a stand mixer fitted with the paddle attachment until smooth and creamy, 1–2 minutes, stopping once or twice to scrape the bowl if necessary. Spoon the filling into a 99-inch (2-quart) baking dish and smooth the top.

In a medium bowl, combine flour, oats, brown sugar, black walnuts, cinnamon, and salt. Mix well, breaking up any brown sugar clumps. Rub the softened butter in with your fingertips until the mixture forms coarse, damp crumbs, about 1 minute. Sprinkle evenly over the filling.

Bake for 25–30 minutes, until the topping is golden brown. Let rest for 10–15 minutes before serving.

_Note: The sweet potato mixture can be prepared up to one day ahead and refrigerated. Add the topping just before baking._
