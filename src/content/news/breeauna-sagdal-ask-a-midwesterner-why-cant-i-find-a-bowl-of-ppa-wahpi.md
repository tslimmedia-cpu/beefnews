**The country's most famous Native American chef is a Midwesterner. **[Sean Sherman](https://seansherman.com), a member of the Oglala Lakota tribe, was born and raised on South Dakota's Pine Ridge Reservation. Known professionally as "The Sioux Chef," he worked for the Forest Service in the Black Hills before moving to Minnesota, where he runs the nonprofit [NATIFs](https://natifs.org) and the James Beard Award–winning restaurant [Owamni](https://owamni.com).

The area where he grew up has shaped America's ideas about Native Americans for generations. As he writes in his new book, _Turtle Island_, "When most people think of the quintessential American Indian, they tend to picture the Plains Indian." By today's definition, Crazy Horse, Sitting Bull, and Black Elk were Midwesterners, too.

So, why's it almost impossible for someone passing through South Dakota on I-90 to find a warm bowl of ppa wahpi, the Lakota bison-and-wild-turnip stew?

Questions like that have motivated Sherman's work for more than a decade, taking him into communities and archives all over North America. _[Turtle Island](https://bookshop.org/p/books/turtle-island-foods-and-traditions-of-the-indigenous-peoples-of-north-america-sean-sherman/0cab3d1820bb54f7)_[, co-authored with Kate Nelson and Kristin Donnelly](https://bookshop.org/p/books/turtle-island-foods-and-traditions-of-the-indigenous-peoples-of-north-america-sean-sherman/0cab3d1820bb54f7), is an ambitious and comprehensive follow-up to his first book, _[The Sioux Chef's Indigenous Kitchen](https://bookshop.org/p/books/the-sioux-chef-s-indigenous-kitchen-sean-sherman/39ec99724be53a61)_, which won its own James Beard Award (and many other honors) after it came out in 2017. It's a deeply researched look at thirteen distinct American regions, from the Gulf Coast to the Canadian Arctic, each with its own pantry and traditional dishes.

In the spirit of the book, I called Sherman to talk about the place he's from, a melting pot where it's a whole lot easier for most people to find imports from Germany, Norway, and other European countries than a truly Indigenous Midwestern meal.

**I think the Plains Midwest is America's most underappreciated food region, and when you get into Native American cooking specifically, you're moving even further beyond what most Americans know or understand. In South Dakota, at least, people can tell you what [kuchen](https://www.seriouseats.com/dakota-kuchen-peaches-and-cream-cake-recipe-11768468) is. The same people might not recognize [wasna](https://www.instagram.com/reel/C33RMJ4un6b/). Why aren't Native American dishes better represented in a state where nearly one out of every ten people is Indigenous?**

Well, it's basically the same story everywhere in the United States. A big piece of that is the history of how Native people have been treated—the segregation of Native people into reservation systems, the stripping away of so much.

Our cultures were dismantled through assimilation and boarding schools, and through the loss of access to traditional foods. Government policy has dictated what animals we can and can't eat. We've had very little access to our own traditional heirloom seeds, and limited access to harvesting wild plants and foods. We were pushed to be forcibly reliant on federal commodity food systems instead.

When you look at that history, you understand why you don't see Native restaurants everywhere, and why Native dishes don't make it onto menus very often.

A big part of our work is to normalize an understanding, across America, of what Native foods are, and to support Native peoples creating those foods and building businesses, opening new doors for what's possible. It's about challenging the American perspective on what "American food" is.

With all the Eurocentrism around American culinary culture, we've ignored the massive culinary diversity that's out there in the United States, and how much knowledge still exists around wild plants and animals and Native agriculture. To me, there's just so much opportunity.

**Following up on that: In the Great Lakes region of the Midwest, actual Native American cooking is still far from mainstream, but native ingredients like wild rice, cranberries, and maple syrup are popular. In the Dakotas, chokecherries and American plums are around, but less visible—and forget thpsila** **[prairie turnip], which you describe as a Lakota staple. Why do you think ingredients from Plains Native American traditions aren't as much a part of the dominant food culture as their Great Lakes equivalents?**

For one thing, they've lost a lot of their range. Consider what happened when Native land uses were replaced with cattle ranching. Cattle disrupted the natural lifecycle of plants and ecosystems that had thrived with bison.

Bison are native to the Plains. They were part of the ecosystem and in balance with the plants. They ate the right plants, helped spread the right seeds, and helped with the propagation of all kinds of foods out there. Then you get this environmental disaster when cattle ranching comes in. European farming practices come in, and barbed wire fences go up. Those fences prohibit animal movements and human movements. They completely disturb how seeds were spread across the landscape.

Thpsila, for example—the area it used to grow in was so massive, and now finding it can be hard, because it was so decimated. It's like ramp populations in some areas, which are almost completely gone because people didn't appreciate them or didn't know how to sustainably harvest or care for them.

**Do you think there's a Plains Midwestern Indigenous cuisine, with common traits, or are Plains foodways too diverse for that kind of generalization? You mention in the book that fifty-five different tribes have lived on the Plains.**

It does vary. A lot of the tribes along the rivers were farming. There are a lot of recipes, like [Mandan corn balls](https://midwesterner.substack.com/p/essentials-cornballs-fort-berthold), that come out of those farming practices. Other tribes were more reliant on hunting, fishing, and gathering.

Overall, my work with _Turtle Island_ wasn't to comprehensively document Indigenous cultures and food systems as they existed historically. I'm more interested in the evolution of Native food systems—imagining a better future where Native perspectives are included in the culinary world.

When we do that, we open ourselves up to so much more plant usage and protein usage, and we have the ability to create all sorts of new recipes moving forward. That's an important distinction: We're not just trying to define what things used to be. We're trying to showcase what's possible.

**We've talked about a few ingredients that define Indigenous cuisine on the Plains—chokecherries, wild plums, thpsila, bison. What else would be on that list for you?**

There are all sorts of wild animals. There are wild onions all across the prairie. In the Badlands, you have cactus, agave-like plants, and other desert-adapted foods. There are so many different kinds of berries, nuts, and seeds around, too. And the Black Hills have a completely different food system than the surrounding grasslands.

**I know you're a native South Dakotan, but as you've said, the Plains region is more diverse than most people think, in both culture and flora and fauna. Was there anything you discovered while researching this book that genuinely surprised you?**

The biggest thing is that there's just too much to learn—more than I can learn in my lifetime. Tackling something as big as North America is massive. Even a book the size of _Turtle Island_ barely scratches the surface of what's out there.

We're not able to talk about every tribe, every culture, every plant, every protein. There's so much opportunity to keep going deeper. Every region could be its own full book, really. There should be a whole book on Indigenous Plains foods, a book on Indigenous Southwest foods, and so on.

I don't know if that's surprising, exactly, but I don't think most people realize how much is out there that hasn't been brought into the mainstream—that people are completely unaware of.

A lot of the recipes in this book probably can't be recreated exactly unless you're in the right region, around the right community, at the right season, and invited into that experience. You're not going to find narwhal or javelina at your local Whole Foods.

**Along those lines, I'm guessing that most of the people reading this interview will not be Indigenous, but some of them probably live on the Plains, around the ingredients and cultures that we've been talking about, and, if they've gotten this far in the interview, they probably want to know more. What do you think is the best way for non-Indigenous Midwesterners to engage with these traditions?**

For non-Indigenous people, a big part of engaging respectfully is understanding what Indigenous people have gone through. There are so many misconceptions, like that we get free health care or free food, and that's not how it is. Whatever we do get has come at the cost of a lot of violence, the dismantling of culture, and being forced into perpetual poverty.

Food can help create empathy. The more Native food businesses we see, the more people will want to support them and start to see humanity across the board, instead of dehumanizing people because they were taught that Native people were "lesser." We're all human. The Native experience brings so much value into food systems, and we haven't fully experienced that yet.

**Right. These are cultures that have developed on this land over tens of thousands of years. Of course they have a lot to offer.**

All Indigenous cultures, in the Americas and beyond, had the original blueprint for living sustainably on the land around them. They had to figure out how to have a balanced diet, with the right nutrients, minerals, and vitamins to stay healthy.

**What other chefs and food figures are bringing attention to Indigenous Plains foodways right now? You told me about [Oglala Lakota chef] [Kimberly Tilsen-Brave Heart](https://www.instagram.com/chefbraveheart/?hl=en), from Rapid City, a while back.**

I chat with her quite a bit. She's in Minnesota now, and she's doing good work.

There's a lot happening on Rosebud Reservation. Cheyenne River has been doing a lot with gardens, and they've brought back bison. Other groups are bringing more bison into tribal communities. There are a lot of initiatives starting to bubble up.

At NATIFS, we're trying to be a support center for people working on these food operations across the Plains and beyond. As we get ready to launch a site in Bozeman, Montana, we'll have another point of support up there soon.

**You talked about redefining "American food." If we're redefining Midwestern food, what Indigenous Plains dishes belong in the canon, alongside kuchen and tater tot casserole? Mandan corn balls seem like a good starting point.**

Sure. There's no reason we couldn't see more dishes like ppa wahpi—soups made from dried meat and vegetables. There's a lot of inspiration in these traditions. I grew up with a stew called tana, made from intestine. That can evolve. There's a version made with bison tripe and oxtail in the book. It's kind of a Lakota menudo.
