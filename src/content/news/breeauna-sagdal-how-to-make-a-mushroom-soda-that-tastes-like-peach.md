**Some customers at [Larder](https://larderdb.com/), Jeremy Umansky's experimental Jewish deli in Cleveland's Ohio City neighborhood, think the chef is lying about the chanterelle mushroom soda.** "It tastes exactly like peach," Umansky says. "People will argue with me—like, _Come on, I know you put peaches in there, too._"

He's used to explaining himself. Before opening Larder, Umansky was the full-time forager and preservationist behind some of the most unique flavors at the James Beard Award–winning chef Jonathan Sawyer's restaurants in Cleveland. At Larder, his approach to counter-service deli fare is just as ambitious.

The team at Larder cures pastrami with koji, the Japanese fungus responsible for soy sauce and miso. ([Umansky co-wrote a 2020 book about it, titled ](https://www.chelseagreen.com/product/koji-alchemy/?srsltid=AfmBOoqUeB-yQS5yTXkBXigUbBHZNqUzIfhU56ltwtu_Ghxg0PpUp5ns)_[Koji Alchemy](https://www.chelseagreen.com/product/koji-alchemy/?srsltid=AfmBOoqUeB-yQS5yTXkBXigUbBHZNqUzIfhU56ltwtu_Ghxg0PpUp5ns)_.) They make gefilte fish from Lake Erie carp. The pickles in their deli case sometimes include invasive knotweed, foraged by the chef or his team. There isn't another restaurant quite like Larder in the world, let alone the Midwest.

In that context, a wild mushroom soda isn't shocking, but the flavor can be.

Experienced foragers already know that chanterelles taste more like peaches than portobellos. "In chanterelles, the mushroom flavor is almost an afterthought," Umansky says. He likes to play up their fruity notes by sauting them with stone fruit—peaches, cherries, apricots, and plums—for a simple summer side, finished with fresh mint. "It's important to show people that fungi aren't just portobellos and earthiness," he says. "They have so much variation and complexity. I think taking a fungus that smells and tastes like apricots and turning it into a soda is a very reasonable choice."

He came up with the soda as a way to use the restaurant's chanterelle trimmings, slowly drawing out their moisture with sugar at room temperature to make a rich syrup with all the flavor and aroma of fresh mushrooms.

For the rest of us, the first step in the recipe is finding the chanterelles. You might be able to buy them at some farmers' markets and upscale grocery stores, but hunting for them in the woods is more affordable and more fun. They like hot days and heavy rain, so they peak in July and August in the Midwest, but they can keep flushing into October. Look for flashes of apricot orange on sun-dappled patches of forest floor, near oak trees. (Before you go out, read up on the differences between the chanterelle and its toxic lookalike, the jack-o'-lantern, which are obvious. "Aside from a slight similarity in color, they really aren't that much alike," Umansky says.)

The payoff is a one-of-a-kind soda that's fruity, earthy, brightened by a healthy dose of apple cider vinegar, and a whole lot more interesting than peach alone. If chanterelles weren't so special in their own right, after all, not many people would put up with ticks, spiderwebs, mosquitoes, and the other annoyances of the summer woods just to bring home a bag. "Believe it or not, when we have this soda on the menu, we can't keep it in stock," Umansky says. "We can easily go through a gallon of syrup in a week. People can't believe it's just mushrooms, sugar, and vinegar."

## **Chanterelle Syrup
**_Adapted from Jeremy Umansky, Larder, Cleveland_

_Makes about 1 quart, enough for 32 sodas_

Umansky doesn't strain this syrup—or any of his soda syrups. That's not laziness. "A good beverage should have texture and body," he says. "Industrial sodas are vapid—just bubbles and sugar. When we use whole ingredients, blended fine but not strained, the drink has character." If you're not convinced, you can strain the syrup through a fine-mesh sieve or a layer of cheesecloth after adding the vinegar.

#### **Ingredients**

2 cups chopped chanterelle mushrooms (about  pound)
2 cups granulated sugar
 to 1 cup apple cider vinegar, to taste

#### **Preparation**

Pack the chanterelles and sugar into a clean quart jar or other airtight container in alternating layers. Cover and keep at room temperature, out of direct sunlight. Stir or shake daily for 2 to 4 days, until a syrup begins to form. Once liquid begins to collect in the bottom of the container, transfer to the refrigerator.

Continue steeping in the refrigerator until the sugar has completely dissolved into a syrup, 7 to 10 days. Blend until smooth (do not strain), then stir in  cup apple cider vinegar. Taste and adjust with more vinegar if needed, for brightness and balance. Store covered in the refrigerator for up to 1 month.

#### **To serve**

Add 1 oz. syrup to a tall glass. Top with ice and 8 oz. cold seltzer. Stir gently and enjoy.
