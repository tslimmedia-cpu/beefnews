_Welcome back to Black Walnut Week, Midwesterner's celebration of a flavor that [the New York Times once described as "somewhere between menthol and mold."](https://cooking.nytimes.com/recipes/1018441-wet-black-walnuts) (I prefer "[damp forest](https://midwesterner.substack.com/p/we-need-to-talk-about-black-walnuts).")_

**I don't have much of a sweet tooth, but there's one dessert I've been thinking about for more than a decade. **The date: November 16, 2013. The place: McCrady's, then chef Sean Brock's flagship restaurant in Charleston, South Carolina, where I was covering food and drink at the Southern lifestyle magazine _Garden & Gun_.

The dessert was a black walnut custard topped with a layer of pumpkin gele and a Carolina Gold rice cracker. That night, I posted on Instagram that pastry chef Sean Ehland had "pulled this one out of my wildest fantasies," and I guess I really meant that, because I've been fantasizing about it since. The whole dish was interesting, but the custard had a powerful_ _nostalgic appeal by itself, taking me back to the black walnut trees of my Ohio childhood and the pudding cups that I probably ate under those trees.

Plus, it really worked. Black walnut is still a popular ice cream flavor for a reason: Like chocolate, it's an intense, tannic flavor that softens nicely in a sweet dairy base.

I tried to imitate that custard at home back in 2013, but I couldn't quite pull off what I had in mind. Even after some adjustments, my version was richer than what I was craving—more eggy custard than silky Snack Pack.

That's where Stella Parks comes in.

The nationally recognized pastry chef and author of the cookbook _[Bravetart](https://bookshop.org/p/books/bravetart-iconic-american-desserts-stella-parks/edc7e517eab5df39) _isn't _quite _a Midwesterner, but living in Lexington, Kentucky, just an hour and a half from my hometown of Cincinnati, she knows black walnuts.

"They're everywhere in the fall, and it's just such a nice seasonal sight and smell," Parks says. "Even if you don't harvest them, you're crushing them underfoot as you're going on walks and kicking them down the sidewalk." She thought she didn't like walnuts of any kind until she made black walnut ice cream in culinary school. "I was completely blown away," she says. "To me, now, it's the _only_ walnut."

Parks also knows nostalgic desserts. _Bravetart _is full of American classics, from retro chiffon pie to homemade Twinkies to a Jell-O-inspired vanilla pudding.

That pudding is a tribute to her grandmother, Stella Hurt, who had a hard-earned appreciation for convenience foods after growing up on a farm in rural Virginia. "She'd seen what it meant to make everything from scratch and knew the toll it could take on a woman's body," Parks says. "When she finally had her own family, she was like, _Thank_ _God for Betty Crocker_." Parks remembers eating Jell-O pudding out of green glass coupes at her grandmother's house. Later, when she made custard in culinary school, she had another revelation: "It was good, but it was no Jell-O."

Parks' recipe uses cornstarch and egg whites_ _(no yolks!) to achieve a light, soft texture and a clean base for real Tahitian vanilla.

With Parks' help, I adapted it to make the black walnut pudding cup of my '90s dreams. This flavor-maxing recipe uses the same cup of black walnuts twice—once to infuse the dairy, once as a candied topping. "Black walnuts are way too precious to throw out," she says. "You can let other nuts go after infusing, but not black walnuts."

## **BLACK WALNUT PUDDING CUPS
**_Adapted from _[BraveTart](https://bookshop.org/p/books/bravetart-iconic-american-desserts-stella-parks/edc7e517eab5df39)_ by Stella Parks_

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!f0qw!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fce98dfbd-9705-45bf-8f21-d512ab50cb8b_4032x2111.png)

](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!f0qw!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fce98dfbd-9705-45bf-8f21-d512ab50cb8b_4032x2111.png)
_Makes about 3 cups, serving 4–6
Prep time: 15 minutes
Cook time: 10 minutes
Total time: 25 minutes, plus overnight steeping and chilling_

**Ingredients
**1 cup (100 g) black walnut pieces**
**2 cups (480 g) milk
1 cup (230 g) heavy cream
 cup (135 g) granulated sugar
 cup (28 g) cornstarch
 tsp. Diamond Crystal kosher salt (if using table salt, use half as much)
4 large egg whites (135 g)
2 tbsp. (28 g) unsalted butter, cut into at least two pieces
 tsp. vanilla extract
Candied black walnuts (below), for serving

**Preparation
**Preheat the oven to 350F. Spread black walnuts on a rimmed baking sheet and toast until just aromatic, 5–7 minutes. (Alternatively, you can toast walnuts in a dry skillet over medium heat, stirring often, until aromatic, 5–7 minutes.)

In a 3-quart saucepan, combine toasted walnuts, milk, and cream. Bring just to a simmer over medium heat, about 5 minutes, then remove from heat, cover, and let steep at room temperature for 30 minutes or, preferably, refrigerate for several hours and up to overnight.

Strain dairy through a fine-mesh sieve into a clean bowl, pressing lightly on the nuts to extract liquid without forcing any solids through. Rinse the nuts, dry them, and reserve them for candying (see below). Return the strained dairy to a saucepan and warm over medium heat until just steaming.

Meanwhile, in a medium bowl, whisk together sugar, cornstarch, and salt. Add egg whites and whisk until completely smooth. Drizzle about  cup of the warm infused dairy into the egg white mixture, whisking constantly. Repeat two more times, then pour the warmed mixture into the saucepan and add butter.

Cook over medium heat, whisking constantly but gently, until the pudding begins to bubble and thicken, 3–5 minutes. Reduce heat to maintain a gentle simmer and continue to whisk until the whisk leaves lines that don't disappear right away, another 3–5 minutes. Remove from heat and whisk in vanilla extract.

Let cool to room temperature, about 30 minutes, then press plastic wrap directly against the surface and refrigerate until cold and set, at least 2 hours. Covered, the pudding will keep for several days. Before serving, whisk vigorously to guarantee a silky texture. (I use my stand mixer with the whisk attachment, but you could whisk by hand.) Then, divide the pudding between 4-6 ramekins, custard cups, or small lidded jars. Top with candied black walnuts just before serving.

## **CANDIED BLACK WALNUTS**

_Makes about 1 cup
Prep time: 5 minutes
Cook time: 15 minutes
Total time: 20 minutes_

These add a sweet, woodsy crunch to black walnut pudding and many other things. I like them on a salad with sliced apple, shredded cheddar, and a creamy dressing.

**Ingredients
** cup (50 g) granulated sugar
2 tbsp. (30 g) very hot water
Generous pinch Diamond Crystal kosher salt
1 cup (100 g) black walnut pieces, from the dairy infusion

**Preparation
**Preheat the oven to 350F (175C). Line a rimmed baking sheet with parchment paper.

In a medium heatproof bowl, combine the sugar and hot water. Whisk 30–45 seconds, until smooth and glossy—most sugar grains should dissolve. Whisk in the salt.

Add the black walnuts and stir until evenly coated. Let sit 1 minute, allowing the syrup to thicken slightly. Then, pour the nuts into a fine-mesh sieve held over the sink and drain briefly, for just a few seconds, to remove excess syrup.

Spread nuts in a single, uncrowded layer on the prepared baking sheet. Bake for 12–15 minutes, until the syrup is bubbly and the nuts are slightly darkened and beginning to smell toasty. Do not stir!

Remove nuts from the oven and let cool completely, untouched, which should take about 30 minutes. The coating will harden into a thin, glassy shell.
