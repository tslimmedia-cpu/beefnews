_Yesterday, Dorothy Lynch would have turned 113. That seemed like as good an excuse as any to celebrate the Nebraskan's claim to fame—her tomato soup–based salad dressing. Stay tuned for three days of tributes, including a celery seed–laced dessert [from the creator of Flucker](https://haterade.substack.com/p/introducing-flucker). _

_For those of you who don't know Dorothy yet, we're starting with the basics, including a recipe for a DIY version of the dressing and another inspired by the best-selling wings at a pizza place in Grand Island._

**The most famous first name in Nebraska food is Dorothy.**

Before it became shorthand for salad dressing, it belonged to a real person. Dorothy Lynch, the daughter of Danish immigrants, was a restaurateur and beauty parlor owner in St. Paul, Nebraska, when she put her signature reddish-orange, sweet-and-tangy dressing on the menu at the town's American Legion Club.

These were the Midwest's literal salad days—the late 1940s and early 1950s, just after World War II, when another Nebraskan (living in Alaska at the time) [created ranch dressing](https://www.nytimes.com/2018/09/18/dining/ranch-dressing-history.html). America was hungry for ice-cold iceberg.

According to corporate legend, Nebraskans went crazy for the dressing from the beginning, bringing jars to the restaurant for Lynch and her husband, Arthur, to fill. Before long, the family was bottling it in their basement. In 1964, they let it go, selling it to a young entrepreneur named Mac Hull, who moved operations to his hometown of Columbus, Nebraska, about an hour away, and eventually built a dressing factory in nearby Duncan. Lynch died about a decade later, [at the age of 62](https://www.findagrave.com/memorial/40337085/dorothy_isabell-lynch).

More than fifty years after that, Lynch's name is on just about every supermarket shelf in the Plains Midwest, and [many far beyond](https://www.dorothylynch.com/where-buy/find-store). She's a celebrity in the same way as Chef Boyardee or Orville Redenbacher—better known as a brand than a human being.

"How she came up with the dressing is a mystery," says Marilea Hull, Mac's granddaughter, who runs parent company Tasty Toppings today. "I've never heard."

Every conversation about what the dressing Lynch created _is_ starts with what it supposedly _isn't_: French dressing. They look alike, and they're from the same tradition of sweet-tart tomato vinaigrettes, but Nebraskans will tell you that they're not the same.

The company FAQ explains that French is usually "oil-based," while Dorothy, as it's often called, is tomato soup–based. That isn't as definitive as it sounds. Plenty of vintage French recipes use tomato soup [the](https://www.thespruceeats.com/tomato-soup-french-dressing-3060377) [same](https://www.reddit.com/r/Old_Recipes/comments/18kqbp6/great_grandmas_french_dressing/) [way](https://www.reddit.com/r/Old_Recipes/comments/s0n4jj/french_dressing/). ([A 1951 Campbell's ](https://vintage-recipes.com/campbells-tomato-french-dressing/)version is notably similar to many of the Dorothy Lynch copycats in community cookbooks and online.) Dorothy [was advertised as "Home-Style French"](https://www.facebook.com/omahaexploration/photos/dorothy-lynch-salad-dressing-advertisement-from-1964-1966-courtesy-of-dorothy-ly/1003364879522760/) in the 1960s. But the tomato soup foundation explains why Dorothy Lynch dressing is creamy, not syrupy, like modern American French.

That's part of the appeal. The other part is a flavor profile designed to light up mid-century Midwesterners' taste buds, with slaw-like notes of celery seed, mustard, and black pepper. "It's just its own thing," Hull says. "A little sweet, a little peppery. It's Dorothy."

In Nebraska, Dorothy is a "thing" with many different applications. It's a pizza dipping sauce, [a drizzle for loaded fries](https://block16omaha.com/menu/), a wing sauce, [a Bloody Mary mix-in](https://www.facebook.com/buzzardbillyslincoln/posts/%EF%B8%8F/1056548649807327/), a flavor-booster in mayo-based chicken, potato, and pasta salads, and a marinade, to name just a few uses. In its versatility and cult following, it's like Cornhusker ranch, except [no one pours ranch on pancakes](https://www.facebook.com/photo/?fbid=805312318266149&set=a.546082104189173). In 2024, the Lincoln, Nebraska–based creamery Ivanna Cone [put out a Dorothy Lynch carrot cake ice cream](https://www.instagram.com/p/C8C2HL3izih/). "It weirdly works," they told the Lincoln _Journal-Star_.

Keeping Lynch's legacy alive has made Hull a celebrity to some of the dressing's many admirers. "One time, I was at a graduation party when I still lived in Illinois, and my friend told someone I was moving home to take over my family business, Dorothy Lynch," she says. "This woman couldn't believe it. She said, 'Can I get a picture to send to my mom in Kansas City? My mom is just going to _die_.' It happens from time to time. It's funny."

Right now, Tasty Toppings is the rare condiment company that's basically betting it all on one product, though they sell both the original "Home Style" Dorothy and a low-cal "Light & Lean" variant. That's going to change soon, Hull says: "We're working on a Dorothy Lynch and ranch combination," making a popular salad-bar duo official. "And we're throwing around doing a wing sauce. Our marketing group came up with a very cute name: 'Hottie Dottie.'"

Lynch, never one to limit herself to a single signature recipe, would no doubt encourage it. She sold Hull a half dozen of her creations, including a poppyseed dressing that Tasty Toppings brought to market years ago. But in the past half-century, one tomato soup–based riff on French has been enough to make her famous on the Plains.

"It tastes like home to people," Hull says.

## **NOT QUITE DOROTHY LYNCH DRESSING**

_Makes about 3 cups
Total time: 5 minutes_

_Dorothy Lynch has seeped so deeply into Nebraska culture that you'll find DIY recipes in generations of community cookbooks. "Some of them are actually pretty close," Hull says, though she insists they never get the seasoning quite right. If you're outside Dorothy Lynch's range, this ought to get you close enough._

**Ingredients
**1 10.75 oz. can condensed tomato soup, such as Campbell's
 cup (150 g) granulated sugar
 cup (120 ml) apple cider vinegar
1 tsp. celery seed
1 tsp. Diamond Crystal kosher salt (if using table salt, use half as much)
1 tsp. mustard powder
 tsp. onion powder
 tsp. freshly ground black pepper
 tsp. xanthan gum (optional, for a thicker, true-to-supermarket texture)
1 cup (218 g) soybean or other neutral oil

**Preparation
**Add tomato soup, sugar, vinegar, salt, celery seed, mustard powder, onion powder, pepper, and xanthan gum (if using) to a blender. Blend on high speed until smooth.

With the motor running, slowly stream in the oil. Continue blending until the dressing is thick and creamy, 30–60 seconds.

Taste and adjust the seasoning with more salt if necessary. Transfer dressing to a jar or other airtight container and refrigerate for at least several hours or overnight, to let the flavors meld.

## **"SPICY DOROTHY" WINGS
**Inspired by [Wave Pizza Co.](https://www.facebook.com/WavePizzaCompany/), Grand Island, NE

_Serves 4–6
Prep time: 15 minutes, plus an optional (and encouraged) 6_–_24-hour rest
Cook time: 45–55 minutes
Total time: About 1 hour, plus optional rest_

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!Sf1p!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Faa6cb399-35c0-4669-ac74-e8753893345e_2069x1552.jpeg)

](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!Sf1p!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Faa6cb399-35c0-4669-ac74-e8753893345e_2069x1552.jpeg)
_Many Nebraskans, Hull included, use Dorothy in their wing sauces. I like the simplicity of the "Spicy Dorothy" at Wave Pizza Co. in Grand Island—a true Dorothy lover's sauce. It's just Dorothy and red pepper flakes, a bartender told me last summer, and it's the restaurant's top seller. The baking powder in this recipe helps with browning and crunch, for oven-roasted wings that eat like the deep-fried wings at Wave._

**Ingredients
**3 lb. (1.36 kg) chicken wings, split into flats and drumettes
1 tbsp. (12 g) aluminum-free baking powder
1 tbsp. (10 g) Diamond Crystal kosher salt (if using table salt, use half as much)
Cooking spray, for greasing
1 cup (240 g) Dorothy Lynch or Not Quite Dorothy Lynch (above) salad dressing
2 tsp. red pepper flakes
Blue cheese dressing and/or ranch dressing, for serving

**Preparation
**Using paper towels or a clean kitchen towel, pat wings completely dry.

Add wings to a large mixing bowl. Combine baking powder and salt in a small bowl or ramekin and mix well. Sprinkle the mixture over wings, tossing constantly, to coat.

Line two baking sheets with parchment paper or aluminum foil and place a wire rack on top of each one. Lightly grease the racks with cooking spray. Arrange the wings on each rack in a single layer, ensuring that none of them touch. (If you don't have a rack, you can cook the wings directly on the parchment paper or foil. They won't be as crispy, but you won't be disappointed. The same is true if you only have one baking sheet. You can crowd the wings a little, and they'll still be good—they just won't brown or crisp as well.)

For the crispiest skin and juiciest meat, transfer the baking sheet to the refrigerator and let wings sit uncovered for at least 6 hours and up to 24. Otherwise, proceed with the recipe.

Preheat the oven to 425F. If you refrigerated the wings, pull them out and let them sit at room temperature for 15 minutes before cooking, while the oven preheats.

Roast the wings for 20 minutes, then flip them and roast for another 20–30 minutes, or until golden brown and crispy. (The cook time will vary slightly with the size of the wings.)

Meanwhile, add Dorothy Lynch and red pepper flakes to another large mixing bowl. Mix well.

Remove the wings from the oven, transfer to the bowl, and toss to coat. Return to the oven and roast for another 2–3 minutes, or until the dressing has reduced to glaze. Remove the wings, toss in the remaining dressing, and return them to the oven for another 2–3 minutes, until the dressing has once again reduced.

Let wings rest for 5 minutes before serving. Serve with ranch dressing, blue cheese dressing, or both.
