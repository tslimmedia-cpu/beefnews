**From the beginning, Cairo, Illinois, has been a victim of expectations. **

The mile-wide intersection of the Ohio and the Mississippi, two of America's great rivers, has inspired big ideas since pre-Columbian mound builders were heaping earth on both sides. In 1818, speculators chartered a city there called Cairo, positioning it as the capital of a fertile region sometimes known as Little Egypt.

The rest of the story is less romantic.

In its early years, Cairo (pronounced _CARE_-oh or _KAY_-ro, depending on your accent) supposedly inspired the city of Eden in Charles Dickens' _Martin Chuzzlewit_, a malarial fever dream of swindlers and scams. The Civil War helped usher in a decades-long economic boom, after the Union built Camp Defiance at the confluence of the two rivers, but that began to fizzle out after an important railroad bridge across the Mississippi went up in Thebes, half an hour to the northwest. 

Later, when the Civil Rights Movement arrived, Cairo, which had been a refuge for Black Southerners during and after the Civil War, didn't rise to the occasion. It all but collapsed in [a surge of horrific racial violence](https://www.legendsofamerica.com/il-cairo/), hemorrhaging residents who left shuttered homes and businesses behind. 

Although a riches-to-rags story is always compelling, the sense of loss in Cairo may be as much about what could have been as what ever actually was—a mourning for what the writer Mark Fisher calls "lost futures" that's in the air all over the Midwest.

Today, the city's practically synonymous with Midwestern decay, like Gary, Indiana, to the north. According to a friend who lives about an hour away, it's being sold for parts, to Sun Belt developers who are using Cairo bricks in new construction.

Still, it isn't quite an "abandoned city," as the first article I read about it claimed. It's home to about 1,500 people, plus a few museums and a handful of other businesses. Its population has halved since 2008, but if not for the morbid attention from media outlets, it might not obviously stand out from all the other Midwestern cities with eerie gaps in their old commercial districts.

There is one thing that's undeniably different about Cairo: its barbecue sauce. 

Cairo-style barbecue sauce is vinegar-forward and _flour-thickened_, with enough body to cling to sliced (not pulled or chopped) pork shoulder.

You can order a bottle of "[Cairo Bar-B-Q Sauce](https://cairobbq.com)" online. Made from an old Cairo recipe in Murphysboro, Illinois, it deftly balances an apple cider vinegar bite with tomato juice, spices, and what tastes like a healthy dose of Worcestershire. 

But for the past seven years, I've been thinking about a more distinctive version that never left home—the one at [Shemwell's Barbecue](https://www.facebook.com/p/Shemwells-BBQ-Emily-Meyer-100063824102392/).

Shemwell's has been open in Cairo since 1946, though you wouldn't know it from the look of the dining room today. A driver experiencing a seizure ran into the building a few years ago, prompting a renovation that stripped out generations of character. 

The house barbecue sauce, fortunately, has maintained its prickly integrity. The sauce has no sugar or tomato to cushion its acidity, which makes it a literally mouthwatering pairing for fatty smoked pork. "This is like eating a Warhead!" my wife, Liz, said when she tried it. It's abrasive and spicy, like a viscous hot sauce boosted with citric acid.

I've been curious about it since I first had lunch at Shemwell's in 2018. In 2020, I asked a Southern Illinois-based reporter to write this piece. He declined—too busy. Last spring, I tried to set up an interview with the makers of Cairo Bar-B-Q Sauce, who I thought might share some insight on the style, at least. It fell through.

The only two recipes for a Cairo-style barbecue sauce that I could find online back in 2018 were on discussion boards that no longer exist. I tried them. They didn't look or taste like what I remembered. That's when I began collecting everything I could find about Shemwell's and the Cairo barbecue sauce tradition in a digital file, including recipes, news clippings, and Yelp and Google reviews. Anytime I was anywhere near Cairo, I thought about going back. Once, I did, but Shemwell's was closed.

Then, a few weeks ago, Liz and I made the trip. We sat at the counter. Talking about the sauce with the two women working, I confirmed three ingredients: flour, vinegar, and butter. I also picked up a couple of jars for tasting.

As I tried to recreate the sauce at home, those samples kept pulling me toward extremes—tripling the cayenne, ditching the moderating influence of sweet paprika, replacing soft, sweet apple cider vinegar with rice vinegar, and then, finally, with white_ _vinegar, which I don't normally use for anything but cleaning and pickling. That's when I got it. If this isn't the exact recipe, it's a dead ringer in appearance and flavor.

It's worth making, but it's also one of a few reasons why Cairo is still worth a visit.

The first time I went to Shemwell's, my friends and I were the only customers, the pork tasted warmed over, and I worried that it would soon be another casualty of the city's long decline.

On the sunny Saturday afternoon when Liz and I stopped by, the dining room was almost full. The pork was so juicy and tender that I ordered a second sandwich for the road—against my better judgment, because I had another lunch coming up. 

I asked about a photo of nine men that was up on the wall. "That's the coffee group," our server said. "They've been coming here for years. They come in every morning and talk about everything—politics, religion" The men in the photo are actually the group's second_ _generation, she said. As we walked outside, another car pulled into the parking lot, and a father and daughter got out.

With a name like Cairo, the city at the confluence of the Ohio and the Mississippi was always destined to be great or an epic failure. It couldn't be just another Illinois river town. Still, despite the echoes of "[Ozymandias](https://www.poetryfoundation.org/poems/46565/ozymandias)" on its downtown streets, it's not dead. And in Shemwell's, it's holding on to one of America's most distinctive barbecue traditions.

## **CAIRO-STYLE PORK SANDWICH
**_Inspired by Shemwell's Barbecue, Cairo, Illinois_

_Makes 1 sandwich
Prep time: 2 minutes
Cook time: 5 minutes
Total time: 7 minutes_

Like other barbecue joints in the region, Shemwell's serves sliced pork on standard-issue white bread and griddles it in a sandwich press, which helps the sauce meld with the pork for tender, smoky bites laced with cayenne and vinegar.

The sandwich at Shemwell's isn't buttery, and I found that buttering the bread before toasting it made it richer than I liked, but you could certainly add butter. If you're toasting without butter, as recommended, use a sandwich press or a nonstick skillet to prevent sticking.

#### **Ingredients**

2 slices soft white sandwich bread
4 oz. (113 g) sliced smoked pork shoulder, from your local barbecue joint or homemade (for the most authentic sandwich, use roughly 1/4-inch slices, but nearly any slices will do) 
2 tbsp. Cairo-Style Barbecue Sauce (below), plus more for serving

#### **Preparation**

Layer the pork on a slice of bread, mixing leaner and fattier slices if possible. Drizzle barbecue sauce over the pork (a squeeze bottle makes it easy, but you can use a spoon) and top with the second slice of bread. 

To toast: If using a preheated sandwich press, cook on high for 2-4 minutes, or until bread is crisp and golden brown. If using a nonstick skillet, toast over medium to medium-high heat for about 2 minutes per side, pressing lightly with a small cast iron skillet or a heatproof plate with a weight on top. The goal is to warm the filling and toast the bread to a crisp golden brown. 

Halve and serve immediately, with extra sauce.

## **CAIRO-STYLE BARBECUE SAUCE**
_Inspired by Shemwell's Barbecue, Cairo, Illinois_

_Makes about 2 cups
Prep time: 5 minutes
Cook time: 10 minutes
Total time: 15 minutes_

#### **Ingredients**

1 cups (360 g) white vinegar
1 tbsp. (9 g) Diamond Crystal kosher salt (for table salt, use half as much)
1 tbsp. (7 g) cayenne
2 tbsp. (28 g) unsalted butter, melted
 cup (30 g) all-purpose flour
 cup (120 g) water

#### **Preparation**

In a small bowl, combine vinegar, salt, cayenne, and butter. Whisk until salt dissolves. Set aside. 

In a medium saucepan over medium heat, whisk flour and water together until smooth. Cook, whisking constantly, until thickened to a paste-like consistency, about 2 minutes.

Slowly add the vinegar mixture, whisking constantly to prevent clumping. (If you end up with a clumpy sauce, you can homogenize it with an immersion blender, or by carefully transferring it to a standard blender, taking care to vent the lid to prevent pressure buildup from the hot liquid. The easier option is to whisk aggressively from the beginning.)

Continue cooking over medium heat, whisking frequently, until sauce simmers and thickens to a thick, glossy texture, another 8 to 10 minutes. Remove from heat and let cool. Store in a sealed jar in the refrigerator for up to 2 weeks.
