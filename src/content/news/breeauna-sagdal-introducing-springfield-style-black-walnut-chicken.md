_Welcome back to Black Walnut Week, _Midwesterner_'s celebration of those pesky nuts cluttering your yards and staining your driveways._

**When I was talking with Jacob Basecke of Hammons Black Walnuts for [our black walnut FAQ](https://midwesterner.substack.com/p/we-need-to-talk-about-black-walnuts), he happened to mention that he lives not in Stockton, Missouri, where the company's based, but in nearby Springfield. **It sparked an idea.

Like many Midwestern food fans, as soon as I hear Springfield, I think of cashew chicken. It's one of America's most singular Chinese-American dishes, created by an immigrant restaurateur named David Leong in 1963.

Unless you live in or near Missouri, this is _not _the cashew chicken you might have grown up eating. Springfield-style cashew chicken has spread to other parts of the state, but it's virtually nowhere else. It's effectively Leong's take on Ozark fried chicken and gravy: crisp bites of white-meat chicken in a brown sauce seasoned with soy and oyster sauces. The cashews are a garnish, along with sliced scallions.

Leong, who was Springfield's first Chinese restaurateur, designed the entree to win over midcentury Missourians. It worked. These days, it's a staple in Springfield. [It's famous enough that, according to Leong's son Wing Wah, representatives from McDonald's once stopped by the family restaurant, looking for McNugget inspiration.](https://sbj.net/stories/leongs-legacy-remembering-the-father-of-springfield-style-cashew-chicken,70074)

It might have more in common with McNuggets than the more traditional items on Leong's menu. That's what I took away from a conversation with [James Chang](https://www.instagram.com/jchang.kitchen/?hl=en), a Kansas City chef who grew up making the dish in his father's St. Joseph, Missouri, Chinese restaurant. "General Tso's chicken has a basis in Chinese cooking," he says. "So do other classic Chinese-American dishes, like chop suey and beef and broccoli. The links to China are fragile, but they're there. Springfield cashew chicken was just this Asian dude trying to make some money in the Midwest. I support that, to be clear. But it's at least two steps removed from China."

My main gripe with Springfield-style cashew chicken is the name. Theoretically, the cashews on top add flavor and crunch. Mostly, they just roll off your fork. They feel like a formality—an excuse to call the dish something other than chicken and gravy.

You can't say that about the black walnuts in this version, a Missouri-inspired twist on Leong's Missouri-inspired recipe ([which he shared freely](https://www.feastmagazine.com/recipes/where-did-cahsew-chicken-originate/article_d7754778-6fa6-11ea-b6b3-63ebcb5a8461.html)) that takes it even deeper into the Ozarks. I simmer half a cup of toasted black walnuts in the sauce, infusing it with their earthy, umami-rich flavor and integrating them into each bite.

You have to like black walnuts to enjoy it, but I think it works. What grows together goes together, right? Or something like that. Eat it with steamed [Missouri rice](https://castorriverfarms.com/products/copy-of-long-grain-white-rice-1?srsltid=AfmBOooLbSbiGjASjPKmZQUmJejp5811BblK12b9amB0uSUZoBu9cOX5).

## **SPRINGFIELD-STYLE BLACK WALNUT CHICKEN
**_Inspired by [Leong's Asian Diner](https://www.leongsasiandiner.com), Springfield, Missouri_

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!BHM0!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F4cbc3676-d863-452f-8249-501a95c71491_1880x984.jpeg)

](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!BHM0!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F4cbc3676-d863-452f-8249-501a95c71491_1880x984.jpeg)
_Serves 4–6
Prep time: 30 minutes
Cook time: 30 minutes
Total time: 1 hour (plus at least 30 minutes for marinating)_

**Ingredients
**_Chicken_**
**1 large egg (50 g)
 cup (60 g) soy sauce
 cup (120 g) milk
1 tbsp. (15 g) lemon juice
1 lb. (454 g) boneless, skinless chicken breast, cut into roughly 1-inch pieces

_Sauce_**
** cup (60 g) large black walnut pieces
2 cups (480 g) low-sodium or homemade chicken broth
2 tbsp. (30 g) low-sodium soy sauce
1 tbsp. (20 g) oyster sauce
 tsp. freshly ground white pepper
 tsp. garlic powder
 tsp. ground ginger
 tsp. roasted sesame oil
2 tbsp. (16 g) cornstarch
2 tbsp. (30 g) cold water

_Breading_**
** cup (65 g) all-purpose flour
 cup (60 g) cornstarch
1 tsp. garlic powder
1 tsp. freshly ground white pepper
 tsp. MSG
 tsp. Diamond Crystal kosher salt (for table salt, use half as much)
 tsp. cayenne pepper

2 quarts neutral oil, for frying
4 green onions, white and green parts, thinly sliced, for garnish (60 g)

**Preparation
**Add egg to a medium bowl and beat until smooth. Whisk in soy sauce, milk, lemon juice, garlic powder, white pepper, and cayenne. Add chicken and toss to coat evenly. Cover and refrigerate for at least 30 minutes or up to overnight.

Preheat the oven to 350F. Spread black walnuts on a rimmed baking sheet and toast until just aromatic, 5–7 minutes. (Alternatively, you can toast walnuts in a dry skillet over medium heat, stirring often, until aromatic, 5–7 minutes.)

In a small bowl, whisk cornstarch and cold water together until completely smooth, making a slurry. Combine toasted walnuts, broth, soy sauce, oyster sauce, white pepper, garlic powder, ginger, sesame oil, and the cornstarch slurry in a small saucepan over medium heat. Bring to a simmer, stirring constantly, until glossy and thick enough to coat the back of a spoon, 5–10 minutes.

In another medium bowl, whisk together flour, cornstarch, garlic powder, white pepper, MSG, and salt. Remove chicken from the marinade, letting excess drip off, and dredge it in the flour mixture, pressing to help the coating adhere. Shake off any excess breading, set the coated pieces on a wire rack, and let them sit while you heat the oil.

In a deep skillet or Dutch oven over medium-high heat, heat at least 2 inches of neutral oil to 350F (175C). Add chicken and fry until golden brown and crisp, about 4 minutes, turning occasionally and adjusting the burner as necessary to maintain the temperature between 325F and 350F. Transfer to a clean wire rack set over a baking sheet and let drain for 2–3 minutes.

Place the saucepan with the walnut sauce over medium heat and warm the sauce. Add chicken to the saucepan and toss until evenly coated. Transfer to a serving bowl, top with sliced scallions, and serve immediately, with white rice on the side.
